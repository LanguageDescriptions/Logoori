Test versions of a Logoori wordlist

Here (links at the bottom) are some versions of a Logoori wordlist that I am developing for scientific usage. That list is designed to be a technical reference work. My present concern is with finding a good way to also present the data in a fashion suitable for a non-linguistic audience, especially Logoori speakers. I would like to test different presentations, and solicit information on advantages and disadvantages of these presentations. Please send comments to Logoori.Project☺gmail.com (replace ☺ with @): tell me what works and what does not work, especially why it did not work for you.

The files basically the same. Each is a single web page with a recorded Logoori word, word transcribed, grammatical information, a simplified stem, and an English translation. For a brief overview of special symbols used in the transcriptions, see this article. If you are reading that page on a phone, there may be problems with the print size of some sections, which I don't understand and I hope to fix it some day. Very briefly, the transcriptions distinguish the Logoori vowels [i,u] and [ɪ,ʊ] where [ɪ,ʊ] are either written as [i,u] or [e,o] is normal spelling. Tones are marked, also there is a complex problem regarding n and ny: the letter [ɲ] is usually spelled ny, however, ny = [ɲ] and [n] are not pronounced differently by all speakers. Finally, tones, which are systematically left out of writing, are included in transcriptions. The transcriptions may be a bit difficult to interpret, but playing the associated recording whould make it clear what the qord in question is.

Here is a simple explanation of a problem that must be addressed in a wordlist, especially in a Bantu language. The word kodéeka 'to cook' has the infinitive prefix /kʊ/, which is pronounced [ko] because of a vowel harmony rule in the language. The root meaning 'cook' is -déek-, and the suffix /-a/ indicates a particular type of verb form. The verb kwááta 'to do surgery' has the same infinitive prefix, pronounced [kw] because of a different rule of the grammar: the root is -át-. The word é!ngókó 'chicken' is made up of a prefix indicating noun class (class 9) which is /en-/, and the root which means 'chicken' is -goko. The plural of this is ɪzí!ngókó, using a different class prefix (class 10) /ɪzi-/. The noun ekegóóngo 'building pole' has a different prefix (class 7) /ɪkɪ-/ which is pronounced [eke-] in this word because the vowel harmony rule is triggered by the vowel in the stem -góóngo. The plural of that (cl. 8) is ɪvigóóngo (no harmony, /i/ does not change, only /ɪ, ʊ/ change). The noun ɪcháage 'grain store' has the same cl. 7 prefix /ɪkɪ-/ but before the vowel of the stem -áge, the pronunciation of /kɪ/ changes to [ch].

Should the words be sorted alphabetically, according to the basic form as given in the list?

é!ngókó
ekegóóngo
icháage
kodéeka
kwááta

But not everybody pronounces the initial vowel of nouns – many people say cháage, kegóóngo. If your pronunciation of the word is cháage, you would expect cháage to come before é!ngókó. The solution may be for you to mentally put that vowel back, so remember to look for icháage and not just cháage. This imposes a burden on you the user.

A possible solution is to sort words according to their stems, leaving off the infinitive or noun-class prefixes. In that case, whether or not you pronounce that initial vowel, you can find the word if you know the stem:

ɪch-áage
kw-ááta
ko-déeka
é!n-gókó
eke-hóóngo

This also puts a big burden on the user, that of figuring out that the stem for 'grain store' is -áge. So for any lexicon which is organized according to an abstract analysis of words to be practical, it must also contain a basic grammatical description that teaches users how to identify the stem. And I do not know if that is practical. However, it will ultimately be necessary, because some words do not have a single basic form (infinitive, or singular). An adjective like -néne 'big' always have some noun class prefix, so what form should be given as the entry for this word? munéne, vanéne, minéne, rinéne, kenéne, vinéne...? Likewise, -táánó 'five'.
